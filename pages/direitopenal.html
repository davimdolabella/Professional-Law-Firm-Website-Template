What is Criminal Law?

Criminal Law is the branch of public law that defines crimes, establishes punishments and security measures applicable to individuals who engage in conduct considered harmful to society. It functions as the last resort (ultima ratio) of the legal system, acting when other branches of law prove insufficient to protect fundamental legal interests.

Thompson Legal Group has attorneys specialized in Criminal Law, offering comprehensive legal assistance, from the investigative phase through trial, ensuring the right to defense and due process for all our clients.

Key Legislation

United States Code

Title 18 - Establishes federal crimes and applicable penalties, defines general principles of federal criminal law and rules for application of criminal statutes.

Federal Rules of Criminal Procedure

Governs criminal procedure in United States federal courts, establishing procedural standards for criminal prosecution from investigation through trial.

Federal Sentencing Guidelines

Provides a framework for determining appropriate sentences for federal crimes, establishing rights and obligations of convicted persons and rules for serving criminal sanctions.

State Criminal Codes

Each state maintains its own criminal code defining state crimes and procedures. In Massachusetts, the Massachusetts General Laws (MGL) chapters 263-279 cover criminal procedure and substantive criminal law.

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do if I'm called in for questioning by the police?

+

When receiving a request to come in for questioning, it's essential to seek legal counsel immediately. A criminal defense attorney can analyze your case, advise you on your rights (such as the right to remain silent), and accompany you during questioning to ensure there are no abuses or illegalities during the procedure. Remember that you have the right to have an attorney present during any police questioning.

How long does a criminal case typically take?

+

The duration of a criminal case varies depending on its complexity, the number of defendants and witnesses, the existence of appeals, and the court's calendar. On average, simpler cases may be resolved in 6 to 12 months, while complex cases can extend for several years, especially when there are appeals to higher courts. Federal cases often take longer than state cases due to their typically more complex nature.

Can I remain free while my criminal case is pending?

+

Yes. In the U.S. legal system, the general rule is that defendants can remain free while awaiting trial. Pretrial detention is an exceptional measure and should only be ordered when legal requirements are met, such as when there is a flight risk, danger to public safety, or risk of tampering with evidence or witnesses. Most defendants are eligible for bail or pretrial release, sometimes with conditions such as electronic monitoring or regular check-ins.

What is a plea bargain?

+

A plea bargain is an agreement between the prosecution and defense where the defendant agrees to plead guilty or no contest to some or all of the charges in exchange for concessions from the prosecutor. These concessions may include reduced charges, a recommendation for a lighter sentence, or dismissal of some charges. Plea bargains resolve approximately 90-95% of criminal cases in the U.S. without going to trial. Before accepting any plea deal, it's crucial to discuss all implications with your attorney.
