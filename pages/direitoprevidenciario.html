What is Social Security Law?

Social Security Law is the legal field that governs the federal Social Security system, which provides benefits to eligible individuals in cases of retirement, disability, and to survivors of deceased workers. Its primary objective is to provide financial protection to workers and their dependents when income is lost due to retirement, disability, or death.

Smith Legal Services offers specialized legal guidance on Social Security matters, serving individuals throughout the application process, appeals, and litigation when necessary, ensuring clients receive all the benefits to which they are entitled.

Our Services

Retirement Benefits

Comprehensive assistance with retirement benefit applications, including calculation of benefits, determining the optimal time to claim, and maximizing your entitled amount based on your earnings history.

Disability Benefits

Representation in Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) and Supplemental Security Income (SSI) claims, including application preparation, medical evidence gathering, and advocacy during medical evaluations.

Appeals Process

Expert guidance through all levels of the Social Security appeals process, from Reconsideration and Administrative Law Judge hearings to Appeals Council reviews and Federal Court litigation when claims are denied.

Benefit Reviews

Analysis and review of existing Social Security benefits to verify correct calculation, inclusion of all eligible periods, and appropriate application of cost-of-living adjustments to ensure maximum entitlement.

Survivors Benefits

Assistance for surviving spouses, children, and dependent parents in obtaining benefits after the death of a worker, including establishing eligibility and navigating the application process.

Special Work Credits

Documentation and verification of special work credits, including military service credits, railroad employment, and work performed under special circumstances that may impact benefit eligibility and amounts.

Key Legislation

Social Security Act

The foundational legislation signed into law in 1935 that created the Social Security program, establishing retirement benefits for workers and a framework for the modern social insurance system in the United States.

Social Security Amendments

Various amendments to the original Act, including the 1956 amendment establishing Disability Insurance Benefits and the 1972 amendment creating the Supplemental Security Income (SSI) program for low-income individuals.

Ticket to Work Program

Legislation enacted in 1999 that provides Social Security disability beneficiaries with opportunities to return to work while maintaining access to benefits and healthcare coverage during their transition.

Social Security Protection Act

2004 legislation that enhanced protections for Social Security and SSI beneficiaries, improved program administration, and implemented safeguards to prevent benefit misuse by representative payees.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between SSDI and SSI?

+

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) is available to workers who have accumulated enough work credits through their employment history and have paid into the Social Security system. Supplemental Security Income (SSI) is a needs-based program designed for disabled individuals with limited income and resources, regardless of work history. SSDI benefit amounts are based on lifetime earnings, while SSI provides a standard federal benefit rate that may be supplemented by some states.

How long does the Social Security disability application process take?

+

The initial application review typically takes 3-5 months. If denied, the Reconsideration stage can take another 3-4 months. If denied again, a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge may take 12-18 months to schedule in many locations. The entire process from initial application through all appeal levels can sometimes take 2-3 years. Certain conditions may qualify for expedited processing under the Compassionate Allowances program.

What is the deadline for appealing a Social Security decision?

+

For most Social Security decisions, you have 60 days from the date you receive the notice (which is presumed to be 5 days after the date on the notice unless you can prove otherwise) to file an appeal. This applies to all levels of appeal, including Reconsideration, Administrative Law Judge hearing, Appeals Council Review, and Federal Court review. Missing this deadline without good cause can result in having to start the entire application process over.

What documents should I bring to a Social Security consultation?

+

For an effective Social Security consultation, bring your Social Security card, birth certificate, most recent tax returns or W-2 forms, medical records related to your disability (if applicable), doctor contact information, employment history for the past 15 years, any prior communications from Social Security, medications list, and if applying for disability, a journal documenting how your condition affects daily activities. Having these documents ready will help provide a thorough assessment of your situation.
